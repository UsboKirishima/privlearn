Common Cyber Attacks

Understanding different types of cyber attacks is crucial for protecting yourself online.

Important: Cyber attacks are constantly evolving. Staying informed about the latest threats is an essential part of maintaining your online security.

Malware

Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems.

Common Types:
  • Viruses: Malicious programs that attach to clean files and spread throughout a system
  • Worms: Self-replicating malware that spreads across networks without user interaction
  • Trojans: Malware disguised as legitimate software
  • Spyware: Software that secretly monitors user activity
Protection:
  • Use reputable antivirus software and keep it updated
  • Don't download files from untrusted sources
  • Keep your operating system and applications updated

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts files or locks systems, demanding payment for the decryption key.

How it works:
  1. Infects your computer through phishing emails, malicious links, or software vulnerabilities
  2. Encrypts your files or locks your system
  3. Demands payment (usually in cryptocurrency) for the decryption key
Notable example: In 2017, the WannaCry ransomware attack affected over 200,000 computers across 150 countries, causing billions in damages.
Protection:
  • Maintain regular backups of your data
  • Keep systems updated with the latest security patches
  • Be cautious with email attachments and links

Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attacks

Attackers secretly intercept and potentially alter communication between two parties.

Common scenarios:
  • Public Wi-Fi eavesdropping: Attackers intercept data on unsecured networks
  • Session hijacking: Stealing browser cookies to gain access to accounts
  • Email hijacking: Monitoring and intercepting email communications
Protection:
  • Use a VPN when on public Wi-Fi networks
  • Verify websites use HTTPS (look for the padlock icon)
  • Enable two-factor authentication on accounts
  • Be cautious when connecting to public networks

Credential Stuffing & Brute Force

Automated attempts to gain unauthorized access to accounts using stolen credentials or trying many password combinations.

How they work:
  • Credential stuffing: Using leaked username/password combinations from one breach to try accessing other services
  • Brute force attacks: Systematically trying all possible password combinations
  • Dictionary attacks: Using common words and variations as password guesses
Protection:
  • Use unique passwords for each account
  • Create strong, complex passwords
  • Use a password manager
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Monitor for suspicious login attempts

Social Engineering Attacks

Social engineering attacks manipulate people into breaking security procedures or revealing sensitive information. Unlike technical attacks, these exploit human psychology rather than software vulnerabilities.

Phishing

Deceptive attempts to steal sensitive information by disguising as trustworthy entities.

Learn more about Phishing

Spear Phishing

Targeted phishing attacks directed at specific individuals or organizations, often using personalized information to appear more legitimate.

Characteristics:
  • Highly personalized messages
  • Often references real events or colleagues
  • Appears to come from a trusted source
  • Usually targets high-value individuals or those with access to sensitive data

Vishing

Voice phishing - using phone calls to trick people into revealing personal information or making security mistakes.

Common tactics:
  • Impersonating bank officials, tech support, or government agencies
  • Creating a sense of urgency or fear
  • Caller ID spoofing to appear legitimate
  • Requesting personal information, passwords, or payment details

Pretexting

Creating a fabricated scenario (pretext) to engage a victim and gain their trust to obtain information.

Examples:
  • Impersonating co-workers, police, bank officials, or tax authorities
  • Claiming to need sensitive information for legitimate purposes
  • Building a narrative that requires the victim to divulge information

Protecting Yourself from Cyber Attacks

Technical Protections

  • Keep all software and operating systems updated
  • Use strong, unique passwords for every account
  • Enable two-factor authentication where available
  • Install and maintain reputable antivirus/anti-malware software
  • Use a firewall on your network and devices
  • Regularly back up your data following the 3-2-1 rule:
    • 3 copies of your data
    • 2 different storage types
    • 1 copy stored offsite

Behavioral Protections

  • Be skeptical of unsolicited messages and requests
  • Verify the identity of anyone requesting sensitive information
  • Check email sender addresses carefully
  • Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking
  • Don't open attachments from unknown sources
  • Use secure, private browsing when on public networks
  • Regularly review your financial statements for unauthorized transactions
  • Be cautious about the information you share on social media
