Phishing Attacks

Learn how to identify and avoid phishing attempts that try to steal your credentials or personal information.

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where attackers disguise themselves as trustworthy entities to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data. These attacks can come through various channels including:

Email
Websites
Text Messages
Phone Calls

Common Types of Phishing Attacks

Email Phishing

The most common form of phishing, where attackers send emails pretending to be from legitimate organizations.

Common Targets:
  • Banking & financial services
  • E-commerce platforms
  • Email and cloud storage providers
  • Government agencies
  • Payment services

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted phishing attempts that use personal information to create more convincing messages.

Tactics Used:
  • References to real colleagues or events
  • Use of your actual name and job title
  • Mentions of projects you're working on
  • Impersonation of executives or IT staff

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attacks conducted via SMS or text messages.

Common Examples:
  • "Your package delivery status has changed. Track here: [malicious link]"
  • "Unusual sign-in detected on your account. Verify here: [malicious link]"
  • "Your bank account has been suspended. Call [fake number] immediately"
  • "You've won a gift card! Claim now: [malicious link]"

Clone Phishing

Attackers create nearly identical replicas of legitimate messages you've received before, but replace links or attachments with malicious ones.

How It Works:
  1. You receive a legitimate email from a trusted service (e.g., a cloud storage share notification)
  2. Attackers create an identical email with the same branding, formatting, and content
  3. They change the links to direct to malicious sites or add infected attachments
  4. They may claim it's a "corrected" or "updated" version of a previous message

How to Identify Phishing Attempts

Red Flags in Phishing Messages

Urgent Action Required

Creating a sense of urgency to rush you into making mistakes: "Immediate action required" or "Account will be terminated within 24 hours"

Suspicious Sender Address

Email addresses that mimic legitimate companies but with slight variations: "amazon-support@amazn-secure.com" instead of an official amazon.com address

Grammatical Errors

Poor grammar, spelling mistakes, and unusual phrasing that wouldn't appear in professional communications

Suspicious Links

URLs that look similar to legitimate sites but are slightly different: "www.paypa1.com" (using the number "1" instead of the letter "l")

Requests for Sensitive Information

Legitimate organizations will never ask for passwords, credit card numbers, or full social security numbers via email

Generic Greetings

Impersonal greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name, especially from services that would know your name

Real-world Examples

Example 1: Banking Phishing Email

From: secure-banking@bankofamerica-secure.com

Subject: URGENT: Your Bank of America account has been locked

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity on your Bank of America account. For your protection, we have temporarily locked your account access.

To restore your account access, please click here to verify your information within the next 24 hours.

Failure to verify your account will result in permanent suspension of your online banking access.

Bank of America Customer Service Team

FAKE

Red Flags:

  • Suspicious sender email (not an official bankofamerica.com domain)
  • Creates urgency and fear
  • Generic greeting instead of using your name
  • Asks you to click on an unspecified link
  • Threatens negative consequences

Example 2: Package Delivery SMS Phishing

Text Message:

"FedEx: Your package with tracking #FDX4729163 is pending delivery. Update your delivery preferences: fdx-delivery.info/update"

FAKE

Red Flags:

  • URL is not the official FedEx domain (fedex.com)
  • Lacks specific details about your actual package
  • Creates a sense of urgency about a pending delivery
  • Shortened or suspicious URL

How to Protect Yourself from Phishing

Technical Defenses

  • Use anti-phishing browser extensions that can detect and block known phishing sites
  • Keep your browser and security software updated to protect against the latest threats
  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all important accounts so that even if credentials are stolen, attackers can't access your accounts
  • Use a password manager to generate and store strong, unique passwords
  • Check for HTTPS (padlock icon) when visiting websites, especially before entering any sensitive information

Behavioral Defenses

  • Verify the sender before taking any action on suspicious messages
  • Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking
  • Never share sensitive information in response to an email or text message
  • Type URLs directly into your browser instead of clicking links in messages
  • Call the company directly using a phone number from their official website if you receive a suspicious message claiming to be from them
  • Be skeptical of urgent requests especially those threatening negative consequences
  • Report phishing attempts to the organization being impersonated and appropriate authorities

What to Do If You've Been Phished

If you suspect you've fallen victim to a phishing attack, act quickly:
  1. Change your passwords immediately for any accounts that may be compromised
  2. Enable multi-factor authentication if it's not already enabled
  3. Contact your financial institutions if you've shared financial information or suspect unauthorized transactions
  4. Run a security scan on your device to check for malware
  5. Report the phishing attempt to:
    • The organization being impersonated
    • Your email provider (for email phishing)
    • The FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov
    • The Anti-Phishing Working Group at reportphishing.org
  6. Monitor your accounts for suspicious activity in the following weeks
